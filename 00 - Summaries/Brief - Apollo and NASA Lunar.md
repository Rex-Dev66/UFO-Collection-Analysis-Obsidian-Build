> [!SUMMARY]
> The Apollo-era lunar observations — the kind routinely cited in popular UAP discussion — which the collection treats as **largely conventional**, identified as such in real time by the crews themselves.

## What it is

NASA's contribution to the corpus is a small set of Apollo 17 (1972) observations plus released visual material and crew debriefings from Apollo 12 and 17. The events are a [[Incident - 1972 Apollo 17 Fragment Field|particle/fragment field]], a [[Incident - 1972 Apollo 17 Rotating Bright Object|rotating bright object]], and the [[Incident - 1972 Apollo 17 Grimaldi Lunar Flash|Grimaldi lunar flash]]. The released [[Source - NASA-UAP-VM Series Apollo 12 and 17|VM image series]] adds six annotated Apollo stills with small highlighted features against the dark sky.

## What it shows

[[Thesis - Apollo Debris Misidentification Pattern]] (*provisionally supported, high*) is the controlling assessment: nearly all of these are attributable to **spent-stage debris, cosmic-ray phosphenes** (light flashes seen with eyes closed — see [[Phenom - Apollo Cosmic Ray Phosphenes]]), **or known instrumentation/film artifacts**, and crucially were **identified as such in real time** by the crews and Capcom. The VM stills are consistent with lens flare, film grain, hot pixels, and point-source stars/CSM. The one genuine outlier is the [[Incident - 1972 Apollo 17 Grimaldi Lunar Flash|Grimaldi flash]], which is logged as **unresolved** and better understood as a candidate [[Phenom - Transient Lunar Phenomena|transient lunar phenomenon]] than as anything anomalous in the aerospace sense. Net: this group is a good illustration of how much "famous" UAP material resolves cleanly on inspection.
